A new layer in the health and life ecosystem

Support Playlists define a new category of navigation.

Support Playlists™ are short, time sequenced pathways that sit between clinical care and the rest of life. They translate what a person says in their own words into a small number of next steps that blend local, national and digital support.

This page sets out the category in public. It is written for founders, investors and system leaders who sense that symptom checkers and static signposting are not enough. It is a marker of authorship and intent, not a product pitch.

Definition

What a Support Playlist is

A Support Playlist is not a directory and not a care plan. It is a small, realistic path that treats navigation as serious work in its own right. It is generated from what someone says about their situation today and it updates as their life moves.

Working definition

A Support Playlist is a short, time sequenced set of next steps, tailored to a person, that blends:

  • emotional grounding and self care
  • local community and voluntary support
  • trusted national resources and helplines
  • digital tools and peer support

Each playlist is specific, kind and possible. It answers a simple question. Given what you have just told us, what are the next few steps that might actually help you.

What goes in

Natural language and life signals

People speak or type in their own words. Triage outcomes, demographic data and behavioural patterns add context. The system listens for demand, risk and intent across clinical and non clinical domains.

What comes out

Three to five next steps

Clear, short actions grouped by time. Tonight. Tomorrow. This week. If it gets worse. Each step links to something real in the local or national system. Nothing is hypothetical or ungrounded.

Why this category exists now

Systems are full. Lives are complex. Navigation has not kept up.

Across primary care, mental health and long term conditions people meet symptom checkers, waiting lists and static signposting pages. Services are under pressure. Citizens are exhausted. Both sides need a gentler and smarter way to match need to what is genuinely available.

For citizens and families

  • They are told to go online but often find walls of links and leaflets.
  • They do not know what is NHS, what is charity and what is commercial.
  • They are asked to complete long forms when they are already overwhelmed.

For systems and commissioners

  • Investment goes into services but routes in are unclear or inconsistent.
  • Signposting tools rarely show who used what, or whether it helped.
  • Clinicians see demand that could have been met earlier and elsewhere.

The macro conditions

  • Ageing populations and rising long term conditions increase complexity.
  • Workforce pressure and burnout limit clinical capacity.
  • Economic strain and inequality push more need into primary and emergency care.

The technology inflection

  • Large language models can now understand natural language at scale.
  • Behavioural analytics can reveal real time demand and friction.
  • Digital inclusion has increased through mainstream tools such as the NHS App.
Support Playlists treat navigation as emotional and structural work, not an afterthought at the end of a web page. The category is possible now because AI has collapsed the cost of curation without collapsing the standard of care.
High level architecture

Where Support Playlists sit in the journey

Support Playlists are designed as a non clinical navigation engine. They are platform agnostic and can live inside online triage flows, hospital discharge pathways, employer hubs, apps, kiosks or voice assistants. The pattern stays the same. Listen. Understand. Sequence. Learn.

Step 1

Listen for demand

A person tells us what is happening in their own words. This might happen in a triage tool, through voice, or in a simple text field. Clinical and non clinical needs are heard together.

Step 2

Generate the playlist

The Support Playlists engine combines language understanding with a catalogue of local, national and digital options. It assembles a small number of sequenced next steps that fit the pattern of need and the reality of the local offer.

Step 3

Close the loop

Demand Shaping Intelligence™ tracks which steps people actually take and whether they return. Over time this learns which paths stabilise demand and where new support is needed.

Together, Support Playlists™ and Demand Shaping Intelligence™ describe a new layer in the stack: non clinical navigation intelligence.

Foundational concepts

The field of non clinical navigation intelligence

Support Playlists sit inside a wider conceptual field. This field can be thought of as non clinical navigation intelligence – the ideas and methods that govern how people move through complex systems in everyday life.

Non clinical navigation intelligence

The layer of understanding that focuses on the life around the symptom. Work, money, caring, housing, identity and belonging. It learns how people actually navigate support and adapts the routes in real time.

The lived moment

The point at which a person reaches out for help. Support Playlists are designed to start from this moment, not from abstract population segments. They assume that how it feels in the first few minutes matters.

Emotional Architecture™

The intentional design of how a journey feels, lands and is remembered. Emotional Architecture™ shapes language, sequence and expectation so that people feel held rather than processed.

Time sequenced support

Organising help by time rather than by organisation chart. Tonight. Tomorrow. This week. If it gets worse. Time sequencing reflects how people actually move through change.

Life signals

The small behavioural signals that show how people are moving through a playlist. Which steps they take. Where they stall. What they return to. Life signals power Demand Shaping Intelligence™.

Stabilising demand

The aim is not to push people away from care. It is to give them a path that stabilises the situation enough that clinical services can focus on what only they can do.

You can think of Support Playlists as emotional architecture applied to navigation. We are as interested in how a pathway feels as we are in where it routes someone.
Category boundaries

What Support Playlists are not

Clear boundaries matter. Support Playlists are designed to work alongside existing tools, not to replace them. The category is distinct from the products and services that surround it.

Not a symptom checker

Symptom checkers focus on clinical risk. Support Playlists focus on the life around the risk. The two can work together, with playlists sitting alongside or immediately after triage.

Not a directory

Directories list services. Support Playlists build a small route through the options. They are editorial and behavioural, not exhaustive.

Not social prescribing

Social prescribing relies on human relationships and skilled connectors. Support Playlists do not replace this. They give prescribers and link workers a clearer baseline path to start from.

Not a wellbeing app

Support Playlists are not a direct to consumer lifestyle product. They are an underlying layer that other platforms and institutions can connect to.

Origin story

Where this work began

Support Playlists did not begin in a lab or a headquarters. They emerged on Portland on the south coast of England, inside one Primary Care Network, through day to day work on access, demand and digital inclusion.

Since late 2024, work in Weymouth and Portland Primary Care Network has explored:

  • a PCN wide digital offer for residents, including QuickLinks and peer to peer campaigns
  • Neighbourhood level signals on what people search for and where they get stuck
  • early Support Playlists for mental health, money worries, loneliness and long term conditions

This has been built by a very small team using off the shelf tools and large language models. The work completed to date is similar in scope to what would previously have required large editorial and analytics teams. AI has collapsed the cost and time, not the level of care.

The concepts of Support Playlists™ and Demand Shaping Intelligence™ are being explored and refined in public. They are held by Neighbourhood CoLab CIC, a small organisation created to test emotionally safe AI and neighbourhood based digital infrastructure.

Originated and developed by Mark Winterburn on Portland, Dorset. This page exists as a clear public record of authorship and intent as the category develops.

Two parallel opportunities

A module that can stand up. A category that has not existed.

There are two ways to engage with this work. The first is practical and near term. The second is larger and strategic.

1. The module that switches on

Design and test a Support Playlists module that plugs into an existing triage or digital front door. This is the implementation route. Stand something up. Watch how people use it. Prove that it works for residents and systems.

2. The category level work

Work on the broader emotional architecture and governance of the field. What it should feel like at national scale when someone anywhere can speak into a device and receive a Support Playlist that reflects their life. This is the path for investors, policy leaders and large platforms.

Whoever defines the category and the standards for non clinical navigation intelligence will shape how future citizens experience help in their first moments of need.
